## 2. Digital Footprint Map

**A. Official web presence** — Visible: domain, brand assets, product taxonomy. Legitimate purpose: customer acquisition and self-service. Misuse: near-identical clone/typosquat domains for phishing. Controls: domain-monitoring/anti-typosquatting services, DMARC/SPF/DKIM, visible "report a fake website" channel.

**B. Online banking / customer-facing brand surface** — Visible: login portal branding, security messaging, app-store listings. Legitimate purpose: enables digital banking. Misuse: fake login pages, fraudulent mobile apps. Controls: app-store brand protection, customer education banners, verified-sender email authentication.

**C. Contact/support information** — Visible: hotline numbers, email formats, complaint/fraud-reporting channels. Legitimate purpose: customer service. Misuse: vishing scripts impersonating "FAB support," spoofed sender domains. Controls: published caller-verification guidance, consistent single official channel, staff/customer anti-vishing training.

**D. Careers/recruitment information** — Visible: open roles, technology keywords, office locations, hiring-manager department names. Legitimate purpose: talent acquisition. Misuse: fake job-offer phishing, technology-stack fingerprinting for tailored attacks. Controls: recruitment-only communication channel, applicant verification steps, minimal internal tooling disclosure in job ads.

**E. Public governance/leadership information** — Visible: Chairman, Group CEO, board composition (as disclosed for a listed entity). Legitimate purpose: regulatory/shareholder transparency. Misuse: executive-impersonation BEC ("urgent wire transfer from the CEO"). Controls: dual-approval payment workflows, out-of-band verification for fund-transfer requests, executive-name monitoring for impersonation domains.

**F. Financial/investor/public report information** — Visible: annual/sustainability reports, financial calendar, ownership structure. Legitimate purpose: ADX listing/regulatory disclosure obligations. Misuse: timing fraud around earnings announcements, pretext for "audit" or "vendor payment" BEC. Controls: finance-team fraud-awareness training, payment-verification callback policy.

**G. Media/news/social campaign information** — Visible: partnerships, sponsorships, marketing campaigns, official social accounts. Legitimate purpose: brand engagement. Misuse: fake giveaway/promo phishing riding on real campaigns, impersonator social accounts. Controls: verified-badge social accounts, takedown monitoring, customer warnings about lookalike accounts.

**H. Third-party references** — Visible: news coverage, review sites, business directories mentioning FAB. Legitimate purpose: independent commentary/SEO. Misuse: information aggregation across sources to build a fuller impersonation narrative (e.g., combining a real partnership announcement with a real executive name and a real support number). Controls: no direct control by FAB; mitigated by strong internal fraud-verification processes so aggregated OSINT cannot alone authorise a transaction.

## 4. Report 67

### Cyberfraud Risk Assessment of First Abu Dhabi Bank's Public Digital Footprint

**Introduction**

First Abu Dhabi Bank (FAB), the United Arab Emirates' largest bank by assets, was selected for this assessment because financial institutions are among the most heavily targeted organisations for cyberfraud globally (Verizon, 2024). A "digital footprint" refers to all publicly discoverable information an organisation exposes through its website, communications, and third-party mentions (Button & Cross, 2017). This assessment uses only passive, publicly available open-source intelligence (OSINT) gathered from FAB's official channels and independent industry sources; no scanning, probing, or private data collection was performed.

**Digital Footprint Analysis and Risk Assessment**

FAB's official web presence at bankfab.com establishes its core brand identity — logos, colour scheme, and product taxonomy across personal, business, and corporate/investment banking. This presence is legitimate and necessary for customer acquisition, but it is also the raw material for brand-impersonation phishing: fraudsters routinely clone bank websites or register visually similar domains to harvest credentials (APWG, 2024). Because the underlying brand assets are, by design, easy to copy, the primary defence lies not in hiding them but in email authentication (SPF, DKIM, DMARC), domain-monitoring services, and consistent customer messaging about how to verify genuine FAB communications.

Customer support and contact information — hotline numbers, email formats, and fraud-reporting channels — must be public for the bank to function, yet this same information is a direct input into vishing and impersonation scripts. A fraudster who calls a customer "from FAB's fraud department," referencing the bank's real published contact structure, gains disproportionate credibility (Hadnagy, 2018). This illustrates a recurring pattern in digital-footprint risk: information that is individually low-risk becomes higher-risk when repurposed as a trust signal in a social-engineering script.

Careers and recruitment information exposes job vacancies, department names, and sometimes technology or vendor references. This is legitimate for talent acquisition, but it also allows attackers to infer organisational structure and technology stack, supporting both recruitment-themed phishing (fake job offers targeting applicants) and more tailored pretexting for later-stage attacks, including ransomware delivery, where an initial phishing email referencing real internal terminology increases click-through rates (Verizon, 2024).

Public leadership, governance, and financial/investor information present the highest-sensitivity category identified in this assessment. As an Abu Dhabi Securities Exchange-listed entity, FAB is obliged to disclose senior executive names, board composition, and financial reporting calendars. This transparency serves shareholder and regulatory interests, but named executives are also the preferred targets of Business Email Compromise (BEC), in which an attacker spoofs or closely imitates an executive's email to request an urgent, unauthorised payment (Federal Bureau of Investigation Internet Crime Complaint Center [FBI IC3], 2024). Because BEC exploits organisational trust hierarchies rather than technical vulnerabilities, it is best mitigated procedurally — through dual-approval payment workflows and mandatory out-of-band verification for any fund-transfer request — rather than through information suppression alone.

Media coverage, press releases, and social media campaigns generate ongoing, legitimate public narrative about partnerships and initiatives. Fraudsters can exploit this narrative in two ways: by riding on genuine campaigns with fake "giveaway" or promotional phishing, or by using legitimate partnership announcements as authenticity cues within a BEC or vendor-fraud script referencing a real, recent deal. Third-party references — independent news articles, directories, and reviews — sit outside FAB's direct control but compound the risk: an attacker can aggregate a real executive name, a real recent announcement, and a real support number from separate, low-sensitivity sources into a single highly credible impersonation narrative (Cross & Kelly, 2016; Button & Cross, 2017). This aggregation effect is the central analytical insight of this assessment — individual footprint elements are frequently low-risk in isolation, but risk increases non-linearly as pieces are combined.

Mapped against the five cyberfraud categories specified for this assessment, the evidence indicates: phishing risk is high, driven by brand recognisability; social engineering risk is high, driven by the credibility conferred by accurate public reference points; BEC risk is high, driven by disclosed executive and financial-calendar information; ransomware risk is medium, arising indirectly through phishing as an initial-access vector rather than through direct technical exposure; and data-breach risk is medium-to-high, primarily through credential-targeting enabled by aggregated OSINT rather than through any observed technical vulnerability in FAB's public site itself.

Protective factors observed include FAB's status as a large, regulated institution operating under UAE Central Bank oversight, which typically mandates baseline security and fraud-control obligations, and the general maturity of large-bank security-awareness programmes. These factors reduce, but do not eliminate, the risks identified above.

**Conclusion**

FAB's public digital footprint is broad, as is expected and necessary for a major listed bank. This assessment finds that the greatest cyberfraud exposure arises not from any single disclosure but from the aggregation of otherwise legitimate public information — brand assets, support channels, executive names, and financial timing — into credible phishing, BEC, and social-engineering pretexts, with ransomware and data-breach risk following as downstream consequences of successful initial-access phishing. Recommended mitigations centre on email authentication, out-of-band payment verification, staff and customer awareness training, and continuous domain/brand monitoring, none of which require restricting the transparency a listed bank is obliged to provide.
