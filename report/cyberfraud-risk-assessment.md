Cyberfraud Risk Assessment of First Abu Dhabi Bank’s Public Digital Footprint

Author: Akan Muhametgali

Unit: ICT111 Cybersecurity Fundamentals

Introduction

First Abu Dhabi Bank (FAB) is a major banking institution in the United Arab Emirates with a broad public presence across its official website, customer support pages, investor relations material, recruitment pages, media releases and social media. These channels are needed for banking services, regulatory disclosure, recruitment and communication with customers and investors (First Abu Dhabi Bank, 2026a; 2026b). They also create a public information environment that can be studied by fraud offenders. This report examines how FAB’s public digital footprint may support phishing, social engineering, Business Email Compromise (BEC), ransomware pretexting and credential-related data breaches. The assessment uses passive open-source research only and does not claim that a technical vulnerability exists in any FAB system.

Method

The research reviewed publicly accessible FAB pages and current external sources on cyberfraud. Organisation-specific evidence was grouped into website and brand information, customer support, careers, governance, investor relations, media activity and third-party references. Each item was assessed by considering its legitimate purpose, sensitivity, possible misuse, likely consequences and relevant controls. No port scanning, vulnerability testing, login attempts, credential collection, private-area access or contact with FAB staff or customers was performed. The evidence reflects material publicly available on 21 July 2026 and may change after that date.

Digital footprint and risk assessment

FAB’s official website presents its name, visual identity, service categories and customer-facing language. This information is low in sensitivity because it must remain public, but it can be copied into fraudulent emails, advertisements and websites. APWG recorded 971,181 phishing attacks during the first quarter of 2026 and reported that impersonation formed 43.8 per cent of observed social-media threats (APWG, 2026). These figures are not specific to FAB, but they support a high general likelihood for brand impersonation. A copied brand can lead a customer to a false login page where credentials, card information or one-time passwords are requested. Likely consequences include account takeover, unauthorised transfers, identity fraud and loss of trust.

Public contact and support information creates a second risk. Genuine telephone numbers, service names and fraud-reporting terminology help customers reach the bank, yet the same details can make a false support call or message sound credible. The attacker may claim that suspicious activity has been detected and then create urgency, request an OTP or direct the customer to a fraudulent page. Research on phishing susceptibility indicates that contextual cues and rapid, low-attention decision-making can affect how recipients judge a message (Vishwanath et al., 2011). FAB’s published fraud guidance is therefore a protective factor because it warns customers not to disclose account details or one-time passwords and gives them reporting channels for suspicious contact (First Abu Dhabi Bank, 2026b).

Careers pages, professional profiles and corporate announcements expose role names, department context, recruitment activity and current business themes. FAB’s careers page also warns that genuine offers do not require applicants to pay recruitment-related fees or use unofficial communication channels (First Abu Dhabi Bank, 2026c). Most of this material is low or medium sensitivity in isolation. It becomes more useful to an offender when several sources are combined. A false recruiter can refer to a real vacancy, while an email aimed at an employee can imitate a known department or supplier. Williams, Hinds and Joinson (2018) found that authority-related cues can influence workplace phishing behaviour. This supports the need for staff to verify requests through an independent channel rather than relying on a familiar name, title or writing style.

Governance, investor relations and financial-calendar information can support BEC. FAB’s investor-relations pages currently publish the 2025 Annual Report, 2026 financial results material and a financial calendar (First Abu Dhabi Bank, 2026a). Listed organisations must disclose leadership and financial information, and removing it would conflict with legitimate transparency requirements. The risk arises when an offender combines a real executive role, a reporting date and a recent partnership announcement to create an urgent payment or document-sharing request. The requested transfer may appear connected to an audit, acquisition, supplier or executive instruction. Because BEC depends on organisational authority and payment processes, the strongest controls are dual approval, separation of duties and out-of-band confirmation using a previously verified contact method. Email authentication through SPF, DKIM and DMARC can reduce some forms of domain spoofing, but it does not prevent every lookalike-domain or compromised-account scenario.

Ransomware is an indirect risk in this assessment. Public information does not demonstrate that FAB is technically vulnerable to ransomware. It can, however, help an offender design a targeted initial-access message that refers to a real project, vendor or role. Verizon’s 2026 Data Breach Investigations Report provides current sector-wide evidence that social engineering, credential abuse and ransomware remain relevant breach concerns (Verizon, 2026). Preventive measures include attachment and link inspection, least-privilege access, phishing-resistant multifactor authentication, network segmentation, tested backups and incident-response exercises.

A data breach is also treated as a possible downstream outcome rather than an observed event. Aggregated public information can improve credential-harvesting messages or help an offender select employees and customers for targeting. The central finding is therefore the aggregation effect: a name, support number, financial date and company announcement may each be harmless alone, but together they can form a persuasive fraud narrative. The overall risk is reduced when no public fact can independently authorise a transaction or sensitive disclosure.

Conclusion and recommendations

FAB’s public digital footprint is necessary for normal banking operations and corporate transparency. The highest assessed risks are brand impersonation phishing, false customer-support contact and BEC supported by aggregated public information. Ransomware and data breach risks are secondary outcomes that may follow successful phishing or credential theft; they are not proven technical weaknesses in FAB systems. Priority controls are continuous monitoring for lookalike domains and fraudulent advertisements, strong email authentication, phishing-resistant multifactor authentication, independent verification of payment and account-change requests, dual approval for sensitive transactions, role-specific staff training and clear customer warnings concerning passwords and OTPs. Public information should be reviewed for necessity, but sound verification procedures provide more protection than attempting to remove all legitimate disclosures.
