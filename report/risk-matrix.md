First Abu Dhabi Bank Cyberfraud Risk Matrix

Author: Akan Muhametgali

Assessment date: 21 July 2026

The ratings below are analytical judgements based on public information and sector-level research. They are not findings from active technical testing.

| ID | Risk scenario | Enabling public information | Likelihood | Impact | Overall rating | Reasoned basis | Main controls |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| R1 | Brand impersonation phishing and false websites | Public logo, visual identity, service language and online banking references | High | High | High | A recognisable banking brand can be copied into messages, advertisements and credential-harvesting pages; APWG reported substantial phishing and impersonation activity in Q1 2026 | Lookalike-domain monitoring, verified applications, customer warnings, phishing-resistant authentication |
| R2 | False customer-support contact | Public telephone numbers, service categories and fraud terminology | High | High | High | Genuine details can make a fraudulent call, SMS or message sound authoritative and urgent | Independent callback guidance, clear OTP warnings, transaction monitoring and rapid fraud reporting |
| R3 | Business Email Compromise | Executive roles, investor material, financial calendar and partnership announcements | Medium | High | High | Public context can support urgent payment, audit, supplier or document-sharing pretexts; success depends on payment controls | Dual approval, separation of duties, verified callback and email authentication |
| R4 | Recruitment fraud | Careers information, vacancies and public professional profiles | Medium | Medium | Medium | A false recruiter can refer to genuine roles and request documents, fees or credentials | Official recruitment channels, no-fee warning and applicant verification |
| R5 | Ransomware pretexting | Public project, role, supplier or technology context | Low to medium | High | Medium to high | Public information may improve a phishing lure, but it does not prove technical exposure or successful initial access | Link and attachment inspection, least privilege, segmentation, tested backups and exercises |
| R6 | Credential-related data breach | Combined website, support, career, leadership and media information | Medium | High | High | Aggregated facts can improve credential-harvesting messages and target selection | Phishing-resistant MFA, anomalous-login detection, least privilege and user training |
| R7 | False investment advertising | Public financial brand, investor reports and current corporate themes | Medium | High | High | Authentic financial language and current announcements can be reused in misleading advertisements or impersonation accounts | Brand monitoring, verified social accounts, takedown process and investor warnings |

Rating interpretation

Low means that the scenario requires several uncertain conditions or has limited expected consequence.

Medium means that the scenario is plausible but depends on additional access, user action or process failure.

High means that public information can readily support a credible pretext or that successful execution could cause material financial, privacy or operational harm.

Critical was not assigned because this study did not test controls, observe an active incident or confirm a technical weakness.

Central finding

The principal exposure is the aggregation effect. A single public item may be harmless, while a combination of a real role, financial date, support detail and corporate announcement can create a persuasive narrative. The most important control is therefore a process in which public information alone can never authorise a payment, account change or disclosure.
