ICT111 Multimedia Presentation Speaker Script

Presenter: Akan Muhametgali

Estimated delivery time: approximately 6 minutes 20 seconds

Slide 1

Good afternoon. My name is Akan Muhametgali. This presentation assesses how First Abu Dhabi Bank’s public digital footprint may contribute to cyberfraud risk. The assessment focuses on phishing, social engineering, Business Email Compromise, ransomware pretexting and credential-related data breaches. It is important to state the boundary at the beginning: this was passive public-source research. I did not scan, probe, access or test any FAB system, and I am not claiming that FAB has a confirmed technical vulnerability.

Slide 2

The method was based on a simple sequence. I identified public information, considered how it might be misused, mapped that misuse to a fraud scenario, judged likelihood and impact, and then identified controls. The sources included FAB’s official website, contact and support pages, careers page, security guidance, investor relations, financial reports, media material and current external research. The evidence was recorded on 21 July 2026. Information from APWG, Verizon and the FBI was used only as general sector context, not as evidence of incidents at FAB.

Slide 3

FAB’s digital footprint exists for legitimate reasons. Customers need online services and support. Applicants need careers information. Investors need financial reports and a calendar. The public also needs security guidance and verified communication channels. These sources contain brand elements, service names, contact routes, recruitment information, financial terminology and current corporate themes. Most individual items are low or medium sensitivity. The risk appears when they provide enough accurate detail to make a false message or call believable.

Slide 4

The first high-risk scenario is brand and support impersonation. A criminal can copy a bank’s name, layout, colours and service language into a false advertisement, email, SMS or website. The message may claim that a card has been blocked or that suspicious activity requires immediate verification. The victim may then be directed to a false login page or asked for account details and a one-time password. APWG reported 971,181 phishing attacks in the first quarter of 2026 and found that impersonation made up 43.8 per cent of observed social-media threats. These figures are not FAB statistics, but they support the general likelihood of impersonation. FAB’s own security page is a protective factor because it warns customers not to share account details or OTPs and provides reporting channels for suspicious messages and false websites.

Slide 5

The second group of risks concerns recruitment fraud and Business Email Compromise. FAB’s careers page warns that legitimate job offers do not require payment for visa processing, medical tests or tickets, and should not come through unofficial channels. This shows how a real careers page can be copied into a false recruitment approach. BEC uses a similar method inside organisations. Public executive roles, investor reports, reporting dates and partnership announcements can be combined into an urgent payment or document-sharing request. The message may appear to concern an audit, supplier, executive instruction or financial event. The strongest defence is not hiding all public information. It is a process requiring dual approval and independent confirmation through a previously verified contact method.

Slide 6

The central finding is the aggregation effect. A public role name may be harmless. A support number may be harmless. A financial date and a press release may also be harmless. When those details are combined, they can form a convincing narrative. For example, a false caller can use a real service name, refer to a current announcement and create urgency around a genuine reporting date. The message becomes persuasive because several details are accurate, even though the requested action is fraudulent. This is why no public fact should ever be sufficient to authorise a payment, account change or sensitive disclosure.

Slide 7

The risk matrix rates brand impersonation, false customer support and BEC as high overall risks. Recruitment fraud is medium because it usually depends on an applicant responding and providing information or money. Ransomware is assessed as medium to high, but only as an indirect scenario. Public information can help shape a targeted initial-access message, but it does not prove that FAB is technically exposed to ransomware. A data breach is also treated as a possible downstream outcome of successful credential theft, not as an observed event. Critical risk was not assigned because this research did not test controls or confirm a technical weakness.

Slide 8

The recommended controls combine technical and procedural measures. FAB should continue monitoring lookalike domains, fraudulent advertisements and impersonation accounts. SPF, DKIM and DMARC can reduce some forms of email spoofing. Phishing-resistant multifactor authentication can limit the value of stolen passwords. Sensitive payments and bank-detail changes should require dual approval and confirmation through an independent channel. Staff training should focus on finance, customer support, recruitment and executive-assistant roles because those functions are exposed to authority and urgency pretexts. Customers should receive clear warnings that passwords and OTPs must not be disclosed.

Slide 9

In conclusion, FAB’s public digital footprint is necessary for banking operations and corporate transparency. The main risk does not come from one public page. It comes from the ability to combine ordinary facts into a credible fraud story. The most useful defence is therefore reliable verification: public information may help someone sound legitimate, but it must never be enough to approve a payment, change an account or disclose sensitive information. Thank you.
